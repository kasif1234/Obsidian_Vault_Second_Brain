### 1. What is a Proof?

- **Propositions and logical statements**: Precise claims that are either true or false.
    
- **Axioms and definitions**: Basic truths and meanings accepted without proof.
    
- **Proof techniques**: Standard methods to logically justify a statement.
    

### 2. Induction I

- **Mathematical induction structure**: A method to prove statements for all natural numbers.
    
- **Base case and inductive step**: The starting truth and the rule that extends it.
    
- **Proving formulas over integers**: Showing patterns hold for every integer n.
    

### 3. Induction II

- **Strong induction**: Assuming all smaller cases to prove a larger one.
    
- **Why some inductions fail**: Understanding hidden gaps in faulty proofs.
    
- **Problem decomposition by size**: Solving large cases using smaller ones.
    

### 4. Number Theory I

- **Divisibility and primes**: Studying how integers divide and form prime factors.
    
- **Modular arithmetic**: Arithmetic with remainders instead of exact values.
    
- **Congruences**: Expressing equality under a modulus.
    

### 5. Number Theory II

- **GCD and Euclidean algorithm**: Efficiently computing the greatest common divisor.
    
- **Euler’s phi function**: Counting numbers relatively prime to n.
    
- **Fermat and Euler theorems**: Key results linking powers and modular arithmetic.
    

### 6. Graph Theory

- **Graph representations**: Modeling objects and connections using vertices and edges.
    
- **Paths, cycles, connectivity**: Understanding how nodes are linked and reachable.
    
- **Trees**: Special graphs with no cycles and unique paths.
    

### 7. Graph Theory II

- **Graph coloring**: Assigning colors to vertices without conflicts.
    
- **Planar graphs**: Graphs that can be drawn without edge crossings.
    
- **Matchings**: Pairing vertices so no vertex is used twice.
    

### 8. Communication Networks

- **Network topology**: The structural layout of nodes and connections.
    
- **Latency and diameter**: Measures of communication delay and maximum distance.
    
- **Congestion**: Performance loss due to limited shared resources.
    

### 9. Relations

- **Relations as graphs**: Viewing relations as directed connections.
    
- **Equivalence relations**: Grouping elements by mutual similarity.
    
- **Partial orders**: Ordering elements with possible incomparability.
    

### 10. Sums, Approximations, and Asymptotics

- **Growth of sums**: Understanding how sums scale as size increases.
    
- **Integral bounds**: Approximating sums using integrals.
    
- **Order of growth**: Comparing how fast functions grow.
    

### 11. Sums, Approximations, and Asymptotics II

- **Harmonic numbers**: Sums that grow logarithmically.
    
- **Products and logarithms**: Analyzing products via log transformations.
    
- **Asymptotic notation**: Describing growth using big-O style notation.
    

### 12. Recurrences I

- **Recurrence relations**: Defining values in terms of smaller inputs.
    
- **Divide-and-conquer analysis**: Modeling recursive algorithms.
    
- **Merge sort recurrence**: A classic example of recurrence analysis.
    

### 13. Recurrences II

- **Linear recurrences**: Recurrences with linear combinations of past terms.
    
- **Characteristic equations**: Algebraic tools to solve recurrences.
    
- **Inhomogeneous recurrences**: Recurrences with external forcing terms.
    

### 14. Counting I

- **Functions and mappings**: Counting by mapping one set to another.
    
- **Bijections**: One-to-one correspondences preserving count.
    
- **Sum and product rules**: Fundamental counting principles.
    

### 15. Counting II

- **Permutations and combinations**: Counting arrangements and selections.
    
- **Inclusion–exclusion**: Correcting overcounts by subtracting overlaps.
    
- **Circular arrangements**: Counting up to rotation.
    

### 16. Counting III

- **Binomial coefficients**: Counting k-selections from n elements.
    
- **Multinomial coefficients**: Counting outcomes with repeated categories.
    
- **Combinatorial proofs**: Proving identities by counting the same set twice.
    

### 17. Generating Functions

- **Sequences as power series**: Encoding sequences into algebraic form.
    
- **Algebra on generating functions**: Manipulating series to extract results.
    
- **Solving recurrences**: Turning recurrence relations into closed forms.
    

### 18. Introduction to Probability

- **Sample spaces**: The set of all possible outcomes.
    
- **Events**: Collections of outcomes of interest.
    
- **Probability rules**: Axioms governing likelihoods.
    

### 19. Conditional Probability

- **Conditioning**: Restricting attention to known information.
    
- **Bayes-style reasoning**: Updating probabilities after observing evidence.
    
- **Probability trees**: Visualizing conditional events step by step.
    

### 20. Independence

- **Independent events**: Events that do not affect each other’s probability.
    
- **Mutual independence**: Independence across multiple events simultaneously.
    
- **Birthday paradox**: A counterintuitive result about collisions.
    

### 21. Random Variables

- **Random variables definition**: Numerical descriptions of random outcomes.
    
- **Discrete distributions**: Probability assigned to discrete values.
    
- **Indicator variables**: Binary variables simplifying expectation.
    

### 22. Expected Value I

- **Expectation definition**: The average outcome over many trials.
    
- **Linearity of expectation**: Expectations add even without independence.
    
- **Simple applications**: Coins, dice, and counting problems.
    

### 23. Expected Value II

- **Expected counts**: Predicting how many times events occur.
    
- **Coupon collector**: Time to collect all distinct items.
    
- **Dependent variables**: Expectation without independence.
    

### 24. Weird Happenings

- **Markov inequality**: Bounding probabilities using expectations.
    
- **Union bound**: Bounding the probability of any bad event.
    
- **Chernoff bounds**: Strong concentration bounds for random variables.
    

### 25. Random Walks

- **Random processes**: Step-by-step probabilistic evolution.
    
- **Gambler’s ruin**: Probability of eventual loss in a fair game.
    
- **Long-term behavior**: Understanding convergence and expected duration.
